The pursuit of artificial systems capable of autonomous self-improvement represents one of the most consequential research directions in modern AI. Scientific and technological progress has historically operated as a cumulative process—each generation of researchers building on the discoveries of predecessors, refining methods, and extending the frontier of what is known and what is possible [Kuhn1962; Popper1959]. Yet this process remains fundamentally constrained by human cognitive bandwidth, institutional inertia, and the slow pace of biological learning. If AI systems could internalize this cycle of improvement—observing their own shortcomings, proposing modifications, and validating the results—the rate of scientific and engineering progress could accelerate beyond what any fixed-architecture system or human-directed optimization pipeline can achieve. The potential societal benefits of such acceleration, from accelerated drug discovery to more robust climate modeling, motivate serious investigation into the theoretical and practical foundations of self-improving AI.

Contemporary AI systems, despite remarkable advances in language understanding, code generation, and multimodal reasoning, overwhelmingly operate within fixed architectures designed and refined by human engineers [Brown2020; OpenAI2023; Anthropic2024]. Foundation models are trained on static datasets, deployed with frozen parameters, and improved only through costly retraining cycles or manual prompt engineering. Even the most capable agents—systems that chain tool use, retrieval, and planning to solve complex tasks—rely on human-specified workflows, handcrafted prompts, and externally imposed evaluation criteria. The architecture that governs how an agent reasons, what tools it invokes, and how it interprets feedback is itself immutable from the agent's perspective. This rigidity imposes a ceiling: the system can solve problems within the boundaries of its design but cannot transcend those boundaries by redesigning itself.

The theoretical ideal of a fully self-improving system was articulated by Schmidhuber in the Gödel Machine framework [Schmidhuber2007]. A Gödel Machine is a self-referential universal problem solver that can rewrite any part of its own code, provided it first constructs a formal proof that the proposed modification will improve expected performance according to a user-defined utility function. This proof-based guarantee is elegant in principle—it ensures that no self-modification can degrade performance—but it is computationally intractable for systems of any practical complexity. Proving that a change to a large language model's scaffolding code will improve downstream benchmark performance requires reasoning about the full distribution of future inputs, the stochastic behavior of the model, and the combinatorial space of possible downstream interactions. In practice, no such proofs have been constructed for systems operating at the scale of modern AI agents.

Recognizing this intractability, recent work has pursued an empirical relaxation of the Gödel Machine ideal. The Darwin Gödel Machine (DGM) [Zhang2025darwin] replaces formal proof with empirical validation: agents modify their own codebases, and the resulting variants are evaluated on software engineering benchmarks. Modifications that improve performance are retained; those that degrade it are discarded. This mirrors the logic of biological evolution, where mutations are not verified in advance but subjected to selection pressure through environmental fitness. The DGM further incorporates principles from open-endedness research [Stanley2017; Hughes2024]—maintaining an archive of all generated agent variants rather than discarding suboptimal individuals. This archive serves as a library of stepping stones, enabling the system to revisit and build upon earlier innovations that may have appeared unproductive at the time of their creation but later prove essential as foundations for more complex capabilities.

The DGM demonstrated striking results in the coding domain, improving agent performance from 20.0% to 50.0% on SWE-bench Verified and from 14.2% to 30.7% on Polyglot [Zhang2025darwin]. These gains were achieved through automatically discovered improvements to both tool implementations and foundation model workflows—modifications that no human engineer had specified or anticipated. However, the DGM suffers from a structural limitation that bounds its long-term potential. The mechanism by which the DGM generates self-improvement instructions—the process of analyzing past failures, diagnosing weaknesses, and proposing targeted modifications—is itself handcrafted and fixed. The meta-level process that drives improvement cannot improve itself. This creates an asymmetry: while the task-solving components of the agent evolve freely, the engine of evolution remains static. The system's capacity for self-improvement is therefore bottlenecked by the quality of its initial, human-designed self-modification procedure.

Subsequent work by Wang et al. introduced the Huxley-Gödel Machine (HGM) [Wang2025], which addresses a complementary limitation. The HGM formalizes self-improving agent development as a tree search problem and identifies what the authors term the Metaproductivity-Performance Mismatch: an agent's immediate benchmark score does not reliably predict its capacity to generate productive descendants. A high-performing agent may produce only unproductive mutations, while a lower-scoring variant may seed lineages that achieve substantially greater long-term gains. To address this, the HGM introduces Clade-Metaproductivity (CMP)—a metric that aggregates descendant performance across an agent's entire evolutionary subtree rather than evaluating individuals in isolation. Thompson sampling over Beta posteriors guides both expansion and evaluation decisions, balancing exploration of undersampled lineages with exploitation of promising branches. The HGM achieves human-level performance on SWE-bench Lite while consuming substantially fewer computational resources than prior methods. Crucially, the authors prove that access to a CMP oracle is sufficient to implement the accept/reject decisions of a theoretical Gödel Machine under specific assumptions, establishing a formal connection between their empirical framework and the optimal self-improving ideal.

Despite these advances, both the DGM and HGM share a fundamental architectural constraint: they rely on a fixed meta agent—a higher-level system responsible for generating modifications to the base task-solving agent. Adding a meta-meta system to improve the meta agent does not resolve this limitation; it merely shifts the bottleneck upward and leads to an infinite regress of meta-levels [Kirsch2022]. For a system to escape this regress, it must be self-referential in a precise sense: the mechanism responsible for generating improvements must itself be subject to modification by the very process it governs. When the engine of improvement can improve itself, progress becomes potentially self-accelerating and unbounded [Lu2023].

This thesis investigates **hyperagent architectures**—self-referential agent designs in which the task-solving agent and the meta agent are unified into a single, fully modifiable program. A hyperagent, as introduced by the DGM-Hyperagents (DGM-H) framework [Hyperagents2025], combines task execution and self-modification into one codebase such that any component, including the self-improvement procedure itself, can be analyzed, modified, and empirically validated. This property—termed *metacognitive self-modification*—distinguishes hyperagents from prior self-improving architectures. Because the self-modification mechanism is not privileged or protected, the system can discover improvements not only to how it solves tasks but also to how it generates, evaluates, and selects future modifications. The DGM-H retains the open-ended exploration structure of the original DGM (maintaining an archive of hyperagent variants as stepping stones) while extending it with this recursive self-referential capacity.

The empirical consequences of this architectural choice are substantial. Across coding, paper review, and robotics reward design benchmarks, the DGM-H achieves performance gains comparable to or exceeding those of domain-specific self-improving systems—despite not being handcrafted for any particular domain [Hyperagents2025]. More importantly, the meta-level improvements discovered by the DGM-H (e.g., persistent memory systems, performance tracking mechanisms, structured self-reflection protocols) transfer across domains: hyperagents optimized on paper review and robotics tasks remain effective at generating improved task agents for Olympiad-level mathematics grading. This cross-domain transfer of self-improvement capability—rather than mere task-solving skill—suggests that the DGM-H learns generalizable strategies for how to improve, not merely domain-specific tricks for what to improve.

This thesis provides a comprehensive treatment of the theoretical foundations, architectural design, and experimental validation of self-improving multi-agent systems culminating in the hyperagent paradigm. The investigation is organized as follows. Chapter 2 establishes the theoretical foundations of self-improving systems, tracing the intellectual lineage from Schmidhuber's Gödel Machines through computability-theoretic constraints on self-reference and self-modification. Chapter 3 examines the transition from proof-based to empirical self-modification, analyzing the DGM's relaxation of formal verification requirements and the trade-offs this entails. Chapter 4 presents the design principles underlying hyperagent architectures, formalizing the distinction between task agents, meta agents, and self-referential hyperagents, and articulating the conditions under which metacognitive self-modification becomes possible. Chapter 5 develops the population-based open-ended exploration framework that enables sustained improvement without premature convergence, drawing on quality-diversity algorithms and evolutionary dynamics. Chapter 6 analyzes self-referential improvement mechanisms in detail, including the CMP metric, Thompson sampling-based tree search, and the theoretical connection to optimal self-improving machines. Chapter 7 investigates archive-based stepping stones and the evolutionary dynamics that emerge when agent populations accumulate and recombine innovations over extended runs.

The empirical investigation occupies Chapters 8 through 10. Chapter 8 describes the experimental framework and benchmark design, covering the selection of evaluation domains (coding, paper review, robotics reward design, mathematics grading), the construction of held-out test sets, and the statistical methodology employed. Chapter 9 presents the core experimental results—task-level performance gains, meta-level transfer experiments, and compounding self-improvement across domains—with rigorous statistical analysis including bootstrap confidence intervals and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. Chapter 10 examines emergent behaviors observed in self-improving agent populations, including the spontaneous development of persistent memory, structured self-reflection, and performance tracking mechanisms that were not present in the initial agent design.

Chapter 11 addresses limitations and open problems, including the scalability of archive-based exploration, the computational cost of empirical validation, the gap between empirical self-improvement and the formal guarantees of theoretical Gödel Machines, and the safety implications of increasingly autonomous self-modification. Chapter 12 concludes with a synthesis of contributions and an outlook on the trajectory of self-improving AI systems.

Three claims organize the argumentative arc of this thesis. First, that the infinite regress of meta-levels in conventional self-improving architectures can be resolved through self-referential unification of task and meta agents into a single modifiable program—the hyperagent. Second, that open-ended population-based exploration is a necessary complement to self-referential modification, preventing premature convergence and enabling the accumulation of stepping stones that support long-term innovation. Third, that the self-improvement strategies discovered by hyperagents are not domain-specific artifacts but generalizable metacognitive capabilities that transfer across tasks, benchmarks, and foundation model scales. The experimental evidence presented in this thesis supports all three claims, while the theoretical analysis clarifies the conditions under which each holds and the boundaries beyond which further investigation is required.
